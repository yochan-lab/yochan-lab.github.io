Beyond Semantics: the unreasonable effectiveness of reasonless intermediate tokens.

A controlled study training transformers from scratch on formally verifiable A* traces. Models trained on reasoning that has nothing to do with the problem at hand match — and often beat — models trained on correct traces. The lesson: intermediate tokens help, but not for the reasons we tell ourselves.

Abstract · in one breath

We train 0.5B-parameter transformers from scratch on formally verifiable A* traces for grid-maze pathfinding — a domain where every intermediate token can be checked by a verifier. Three things happen that shouldn't. Models produce correct plans from invalid traces. Models trained on traces from unrelated problems generalize better than models trained on correct traces. Reinforcement learning raises accuracy while lowering trace validity. Taken together, the results argue that so-called "Chains of Thought" do not necessarily mean what they seem to mean.

§ 01 — Setup

A gridworld where every thought can be checked.

Pre-trained models make trace-validity a matter of interpretation — natural-language traces are too ambiguous to formally audit. So we shrink the problem until it becomes auditable: 30×30 mazes, four orthogonal actions, a vocabulary of 944 tokens, and an A* validator that replays every create/close operation against an open and closed list.

§ 02 — Findings

Four results that should not be true.

Finding 01
Correct plans, invalid traces.
Trace validity ≠ solution correctness.
Correct-trace-trained models still produce correct plans from invalid traces: up to 8% of correct plans for the Wilson model and up to 19% for the SF-style model. On the hardest length-generalization bin, only 52% of correct plans come from valid traces. If traces drove solutions, this number would be 100.
Finding 02
Swapped beats correct.
Irrelevant traces match or outperform real ones.
Train models on traces from other mazes (the "Swapped" setup) and accuracy doesn't drop — it rises on OOD. On the cave-like Drunkard set, the SF-style Swapped model hits 95.4% where the correct-trace model gets 62.1%. Trace validity: 0.0%.
Finding 03
RL widens the gap.
GRPO improves accuracy while eroding traces.
Post-training the SF-style correct-trace model with GRPO pushes plan accuracy up and trace validity down from 89% to 52%. The Swapped models keep improving too — still at 0% trace validity throughout.
Finding 04
Length is not thinking.
Trace length ⊥ problem complexity.
Pearson correlation between generated trace length and ground-truth A* length: r = 0.0081 for Wilson, r = 0.02 for Wilson-model evaluated on OOD SF-style mazes. Longer traces are not "inference-time scaling" — they're sampling noise.

The U-shape: how much swapping is too much?

Instead of fully replacing the trace distribution, we sweep the proportion of examples receiving mismatched traces. A little swapping hurts — a lot of swapping helps. Plan accuracy traces a U; trace validity monotonically collapses. Wilson-trained, 50k samples.

Wilson Kruskal DFS Drunkard

(a) Plan accuracy vs % swapped traces

Add 25% swap: accuracy dips. Reach 75–100% swap: accuracy climbs back — and exceeds the normal baseline on Drunkard (OOD).

(b) Trace validity vs % swapped traces

Trace validity falls monotonically from ~97% (Normal) to 0% (fully Swapped). No U-shape here — accuracy and trace-meaning have decoupled.

What matters for transformers to improve accuracy with intermediate tokens is not their semantic correctness, but rather their consistency.

§ 04 — Trace length

Longer traces are not harder thinking.

A popular reading of CoT length says models "think longer" on harder problems — "inference-time scaling." If that were true, model trace length would track A*'s ground-truth trace length. It doesn't.

Wilson model · in-distribution (Wilson tests)

Pearson r = 0.40 Spearman ρ = 0.98

Wilson model · out-of-distribution (SF-style)

Pearson r = 0.02 Spearman ρ = 0.02

→ In-distribution, the model learned to imitate the length distribution of its training data, not the computation. One maze-generator further out, that illusion collapses.

§ 05 — Implications

What the traces are not.

I
"Chain of Thought" is not a chain of thought.
Intermediate tokens help the model — that much is real and reproducible. But in our fully controlled setting, they help without needing to be about the problem they accompany. Anthropomorphic readings ("aha" moments, deliberation, stepwise reasoning) require evidence beyond surface form.
II
Consistency, not meaning, drives the gain.
A U-shaped curve in accuracy as we sweep swap-rate from 0% → 100% tells the story: well-structured but irrelevant traces work. Well-structured and correct traces also work. What does not work is inconsistent traces. The useful quantity is statistical regularity of the intermediate stream, not its semantic fidelity.
III
Think of intermediate tokens as prompt augmentations.
A reframing: the model is learning a Skolem function PA = fθ(T, LLM) that augments the task prompt into a form the base distribution handles better. Prompt augmentations have no need to be human-interpretable — and our results suggest they usually aren't.
